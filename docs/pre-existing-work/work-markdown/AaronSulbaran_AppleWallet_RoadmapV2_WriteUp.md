# Roadmap v2: Theme-Based, Outcome-Anchored

## Why a second roadmap

The v1 roadmap, built in Productboard, is a six-month time-based release plan. It answers the question "when does each feature ship?" That format is useful for engineering execution and stakeholder check-ins, but it does not communicate strategy. Class 18 explicitly defines a roadmap as something that "describes output intended to achieve your product vision" and "focuses on the value you propose to deliver." The v1 roadmap describes output. It does not describe value.

The v2 roadmap is structured around the format Ditson holds up as the more sophisticated alternative: a theme-based roadmap. Instead of organizing features by month, v2 organizes them by the strategic outcome they ladder up to. Each theme has a measurable target, and every feature on the roadmap is there because it moves that specific number.

## The three themes

The themes are not invented. They are the three input clusters defined in the North Star Metric deliverable: Breadth, Reliability, and Frequency. Each one already has a target metric attached.

**Theme 1: Breadth.** Get more credentials in Wallet so users have more daily moments of use. Target: full-suite credential adoption from 18% to 35%. Features: Guided Wallet Setup (now), Health Insurance Credentials and Credential Import Scanner (next), Campus and Workplace Badges and International Compatibility (later).

**Theme 2: Reliability.** Make every tap succeed so users trust Wallet over the physical card. Target: surfacing accuracy from 72% to 92%. Features: Contextual Smart Surfacing (now), Offline Transaction Mode (next).

**Theme 3: Frequency.** Drive repeat use across daily life so Wallet becomes habituated, not occasional. Target: physical wallet abandonment from 0% to 25% of power users. Features: Merchant Acceptance Indicator (now), Wallet Organization and Custom Grouping (next), Peer-to-Peer Identity Verification (later).

## Three horizons instead of six months

The v1 timeline reads month by month from July to December. The v2 roadmap collapses that into three horizons: Now (Q3 2026), Next (Q4 2026), and Later (2027). This matches the "Daily Driver" structure from Class 20: vision sets rarely, business goals shift quarterly, feature rollout is rolling. A horizon view communicates commitment level honestly. The Now column is real work. The Next column is planned. The Later column is directional and explicitly not committed, which is more truthful than a Productboard card stretching across November and December.

## What v2 makes visible that v1 hides

The v1 roadmap distributes features across months with no visible logic. Why is Health Insurance in September rather than August? The format does not say. The v2 roadmap exposes the reasoning: the Now column is intentionally light (Class 18: "you can't do all priority 1 large at once"), the Reliability theme has a deliberately empty Later cell because the foundational work belongs early, and the Later column is reserved for items that are either gated by external partners (Campus Badges, International) or still need discovery validation (Peer-to-Peer Identity).

Every feature card carries one short sentence connecting it back to its RICE rationale or its strategic role: "RICE #1," "Highest reach (300M/mo)," "Replaces a wallet category," "Lowest confidence (50%) — viability validated before commit." This turns the roadmap into something a stakeholder can read top-to-bottom and understand the strategy from, not just the schedule.

## How the two roadmaps work together

Both roadmaps appear in the portfolio. The v1 Productboard view stays as evidence of the assignment as originally specified: a tool-based, time-sequenced roadmap built in the same software a working PM would use. The v2 roadmap sits above it as the strategic communication artifact, the one you would show in a leadership review or on a customer call. They are the same ten features, the same priorities from the RICE and Kano work, organized for different audiences and different conversations.
