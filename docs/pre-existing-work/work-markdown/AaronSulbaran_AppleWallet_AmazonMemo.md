---
to: Tim Cook, Greg Joswiak, Craig Federighi, Luca Maestri
from: Aaron Sulbaran, Product Manager, Apple Wallet
date: May 2026
re: Restructuring Apple Wallet Around Credential Categories, Starting with Healthcare
---

In 2014, Apple started a task that stemmed from an impossible idea at the time: replacing the physical wallet entirely. Since then, the task has turned into an entire product line that over 800 million users use in their day-to-day life via Apple Wallet. Now, we have to move beyond just replacing physical payment cards that our users hold, but rather all of the credentials in a secure, safe manner. I am recommending that Apple Wallet formally restructure its product strategy and roadmap around credential categories rather than a flat backlog of card types, with healthcare becoming the first category we commit to in FY27. This is a two-part bet: the immediate move is shipping health insurance credential integration as a flagship feature, and the structural move is reorganizing how Wallet plans, staffs, and ships every credential and future feature that follows. The goal is to position Wallet as Apple's identity layer that serves every type of user rather than a payments app with extras.

Wallet today is the most-used mobile wallet in the United States, with roughly 800 million active users globally, around 50% U.S. mobile wallet share, and approximately $7.6 trillion in annual transaction volume routed through the platform. Those are extraordinary numbers, and they obscure a problem. Only about 18% of users actively engage with three or more credential types. The other 82% are using Wallet as a payment app with occasional automated extras. This means that the strategic intent of replacing the physical wallet has stalled at one credential category, payment cards, while the credentials that still keep a leather wallet in someone's back pocket, insurance, identity, workplace access, sit outside the platform.

The current roadmap will not close that gap because it is structured to add cards one at a time rather than to enter categories users explore one at a time. We treat a state ID, a hotel key, and a transit pass as parallel line items in the same backlog, when in reality each one belongs to a different credential category with different partners, regulatory surface, and user journey. A guiding tenet for this restructure is how we organize the roadmap around the credentials users carry, not the integrations engineering happens to be building. The belief sounds simple, but it is the difference between a feature strategy and a feature factory.

The argument for a categories model with room for future credentials integration is that it changes how Wallet competes. A card-by-card roadmap means every new credential is a one-off negotiation, a one-off design pattern, and a one-off engineering effort. A category model means we build reusable infrastructure once, the verification flows, the issuer onboarding, the user consent and revocation patterns, and then ship the second, third, and fourth credential in a category at a fraction of the cost. The first category establishes that infrastructure, and the same infrastructure extends naturally into workplace access (badges, building entry, expense credentials), peer-to-peer identity (age verification, professional licenses, vaccination records), and credential types we have not yet explored or that haven't even been created. This compounds in a way a flat backlog cannot. Each new category we enter strengthens Wallet's position as the default digital credential surface, which strengthens the pitch to the next category's issuers, lowering the cost of entering the category after that. Payments took ten years to reach 50% market share because we entered alone. Healthcare, workplace, and identity will move faster because each one rides on the trust and ubiquity our existing product has built. The flywheel does not exist if we keep adding cards one at a time; it exists the moment we organize around categories and let each one reinforce the next.

Healthcare is the right place to prove the model because it is the credential category with the highest friction in the existing user base and the clearest path to partner alignment. I previously shared a customer letter from Marcus Reeves, a 27-year-old consultant in New York who has not opened his physical wallet in months. This user reveals a powerful pattern found across Wallet power users everywhere. People that would otherwise use Wallet for everything in their daily lives are forced back to paper at urgent care, pharmacies, or other run-ins with healthcare institutions where his insurance card is the last credential he carries. That friction is not a niche complaint. It is the most common reason a high-engagement user reaches for a physical wallet at all, which makes it the highest-leverage moment to convert. Health insurance credential integration scored as a Must Have, Large feature in our prioritization work and ranked among the top features in our RICE analysis, which validates the demand signal. The partner side is also more navigable than one might think. The major payer ecosystem is concentrated, the standards (FHIR, SMART Health Cards) are mature, and Apple has already built the trust posture, biometric authentication, Secure Enclave, on-device verification, and the trust and security that payers require to release credentials to a phone.

The risks worth naming are regulatory complexity in healthcare (HIPAA, state-by-state insurance regulations), the partner concentration risk if a top-three payer declines to participate at launch, and the internal cost of restructuring teams around categories rather than features. None of these change the recommendation. The regulatory work is required for any healthcare entry, whether we treat it as a category or a one-off card, and tackling it now amortizes the cost across every credential we add later. Partner risk is mitigated by piloting with a single national payer in two markets before broad rollout, which is a smaller commitment than the partner motions we have already executed for state IDs. The reorganization cost is real but is the kind of structural investment that has a defined end point, not an ongoing tax.

I am asking for three decisions. First, alignment from this group that the Wallet roadmap will be restructured around credential categories starting in FY27, with healthcare as the first committed category. Second, staffing approval for a dedicated healthcare credentials pod (PM, design lead, engineering lead, partnerships lead, plus a working team) with a target of feature availability in supported U.S. markets by end of Q3 FY27 and a 12% activation rate among Wallet users in those markets within 90 days of launch. Third, a commitment to revisit the credential portfolio at the end of FY27 to select the second category, with workplace access and peer-to-peer identity as the leading candidates. The decision in front of us is whether Wallet remains the best payment app on the phone or becomes the identity layer for everything a person carries. I strongly recommend sticking to our decade long vision and push for the latter.
