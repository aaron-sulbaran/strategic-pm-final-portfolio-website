# Customer Letter: Apple Wallet

**From:** Marcus Reeves, New York, NY
**To:** Tim Cook, CEO, Apple Inc.

---

Dear Mr. Cook,

I'm writing because I recently realized something that caught me off guard: I haven't opened my physical wallet in over four months. It's sitting in a drawer in my apartment, and I genuinely forgot it was there. That realization felt worth sharing, because it didn't happen overnight. It happened because Apple Wallet quietly became the most reliable thing in my daily routine.

I'm 27 and I work as a consultant in Manhattan. My days move fast. I'm on and off the subway, in and out of client offices, grabbing coffee between meetings, catching flights out of LaGuardia or JFK a few times a month. A year ago, all of that still required me to manage a small inventory of physical things: a MetroCard, a work badge, my driver's license, two credit cards, an insurance card, and whatever loyalty cards I'd stuffed in my back pocket. If I forgot my wallet, my whole day was disrupted. If my phone died, I still had a fallback. But it was clunky, redundant, and honestly felt outdated for someone who does everything else on their phone.

What changed for me was the latest version of Apple Wallet. I added my New York driver's license when it became available, which was the tipping point. Once my ID was on my phone, the last reason to carry a physical wallet disappeared. But it wasn't just the ID. It was the way everything started working together. My transit card triggers automatically when I walk into a station. My office building recognizes my digital badge through my Apple Watch without me pulling anything out of my pocket. When I fly, my boarding pass surfaces on my lock screen at the right time based on where I am. My Apple Card gives me real-time spending breakdowns that actually help me budget instead of just tracking what I already spent.

The security side of it is what made me trust the switch completely. Knowing that every transaction is authenticated through Face ID and that my actual card numbers are never shared with merchants gave me more confidence than I ever had handing a physical card to a stranger. When I lost my phone briefly at a conference last month, I was able to lock everything instantly from my Watch. Nothing was compromised. If I had lost a physical wallet with my license, credit cards, and transit pass, the recovery process would have taken weeks.

If I could share one thought on where Apple Wallet could keep pushing, it would be this: the few moments where I still hit friction are the ones where the physical world hasn't caught up yet. I went to an urgent care clinic last month and still had to dig through my email for my insurance information and fill out paper forms with details that my phone already knows. I had a similar experience at a pharmacy, where I had to read my member ID off a physical card I didn't have with me but I had a picture of it (thankfully). If Apple Wallet could become the place where my health insurance credentials, my HSA card, and my verified identity all come together for a provider to read in one tap, that would close the last real gap in my daily life. The pattern is already there with transit, payments, and building access. Healthcare just hasn't been connected to it yet.

I know this letter probably sounds insignificant. But the compound effect of removing all that daily friction has genuinely improved my quality of life. I'm faster through airports. I never pat my pockets anxiously before leaving my apartment. I don't worry about losing things that are difficult to replace. Apple Wallet didn't just digitize what was in my pocket; it made the whole concept of carrying credentials feel seamless in a way that nothing else I've tried has matched.

Thank you for building something that works this well without demanding my attention. That's the part that impressed me most. I don't think about Apple Wallet during my day. I just live my day, and it keeps up.

Sincerely,
Marcus Reeves
